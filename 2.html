Lesson 2: Nominative, Accusative & Genitive Nouns

If you're familiar with noun declensions, you probably know that many languages base them off of the gender of a noun (e.g. Russian, Latin, French, et cerera). However, in Kavrėl, the declension a noun falls into simply depends on its root ending. Each declension is made up of eleven cases (all of which shall be covered in time. It's easier than it seems, trust me), & five numbers. To elaborate on the latter, English contains two: singular & plural. In Kavrėl, alongside these, we use paucal (a few of a thing, relative to the whole), relevant plural (every one of a thing that is relevant to a statement, context is almost always needed), & absolute plural (every one of a thing that exists).

In terms of the relevant & absolute plurality, the plural number is used, and a particle marks this! (A guide on particles will come soon.) Also, the nominative singular never uses an affix, regardless of declension.

The Genitive Case

In general, the genitive case is used to mark one word (often a noun) modifying another. In English, the two types of genitives that are explicitly marked are the Saxon- (x's y, posessive) and the periphrasic- (x of y, prepositional). Here, we'll be using three:

- The posessive genitive (GEN.POSS) is used to show ownership or association (e.g. "John's book").

- The attributive genitive (GEN.ATTR) is used to define/classify the head noun, or show a characteristic (e.g. "House of Leaves," "summer's night")

- The objective genitive (GEN.OBJ) is a weird one. It is used when the genitive noun functions as the object of an implied action expressed by the head noun, but within a nominalized noun phrase. For example, “the fear of death” functions as a single noun phrase, but “death” is the thing being feared. Thus, it can be paraphrased as “death is feared.” On the homepage, I gave a translation which included “traitor-VOC self-GEN.OBJ.” It does not refer to someone who has betrayed an entity called “the self,” but instead treats “traitor of the self” as a compressed noun phrase meaning one who betrays the self, where “self” functions as the object of the implied verb “betray.”

Nominative And Accusative

Moving on, the remaining two cases are quite a bit easier to cover. In a given sentence, the subject (nominative) performs an action, & the direct object (accusative) receives an action. I'll give you an example, but it may not be necessary. If I were to say "he loves cats," "he" is nominative because it is the thing that loves, & "cats" is accusative because they are the things being loved.

Earlier, I mentioned noun phrases & head nouns. A noun phrase (abbreviated & glossed as NP) is a group of words centered on a single head noun; this can range in size from the head noun "cat," to something such as "a general statement," where "statement" acts as the head noun & all other elements in the NP are dependent on it, to something far larger, such as "that exceedingly obvious truth which you cannot, for the life of you, accept, no matter the degree at which your feigned ignorance boosts your self-esteem when it comes to coping with this issue" (here, "truth" is the head noun; despite reaching a length of 34 words, it still functions as a single NP). But I digress; in Kavrėl, each NP is generally prefaced by "ta," for the sake of coherency. There are several different alternatives, mainly used in terms of animacy and whether or not it's a proper noun, but for now, don't worry about it.

Recap

For the last 3, x is the head noun and y is the genitive.
Case Example
NOM x affects y
ACC x is affected by y
GEN.POSS y has/owns x
GEN.ATTR y characterizes x
GEN.OBJ x targets y

Examples

ta tre ezam.
NP earth-NOM center-GEN.POSS
"The Earth's core."
ta tyn atynyvra.
NP warmth-NOM fire-GEN.ATTR
"The warmth of the fire."
ta tanet milinyf.
NP experience-NOM odd-GEN.OBJ
“The experience of existing in a state where one's surroundings are misaligned with oneself.”

Lexical Practice

Translate:

"ta lydz etvyhe (was spilt)."
"atyn ezyv (can lead to situations which produce) tynes"
The rat's water was spilt.
Earth's warmth can lead to fires.
